Norse mythology is the ancient belief system of the Norse people — the seafaring tribes of Scandinavia, including the Vikings of Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Iceland. This mythology was passed down orally for centuries before being written down in the 13th century. At the heart of Norse mythology is a rich and symbolic world filled with gods, giants, dwarves, elves, and monsters. Unlike many other mythologies, the Norse did not view their gods as immortal or all-powerful. Instead, their deities were fallible, complex, and bound by fate — even the mighty Odin and Thor were destined to die in the end-time battle known as Ragnarök. The earliest written records of these myths come from two medieval texts: The Poetic Edda: A collection of traditional Norse poems.

Norse mythology, rooted in the pre-Christian beliefs of Scandinavia, is a rich tapestry of stories about gods, goddesses, giants, monsters, and heroes. It explains the creation of the world, the cycle of life and death, and the eventual destruction and renewal of the cosmos, all centered around the concept of Yggdrasil, the world tree.

FACTS OF NORSE MYTHOLOGY

Norse mythology, the belief system of Germanic peoples in Scandinavia, is a rich tapestry of stories about gods, goddesses, giants, dwarves, elves, and other mythical creatures. These tales, primarily shared orally and later recorded in texts like the Prose Edda, explain the creation of the world, the structure of the cosmos, and the eventual destruction and rebirth of the world in Ragnarok.

GODS & GODDESSES

HEIMDALL & BRAGI

Heimdall, in Norse mythology, is the watchman of the gods, guarding the rainbow bridge Bifröst that connects Asgard to other realms. He is known for his exceptional senses, particularly his hearing and sight, allowing him to perceive threats from afar.Bragi is the Norse god of poetry, eloquence, and wisdom. He is known for his skill in reciting verses and his knowledge of skaldic poetry, the traditional verse form of the Vikings. Bragi is also associated with the mead of poetry and is sometimes depicted with a harp.

TY'R & HEL

Tyr and Hel are significant figures in Norse mythology. Tyr is the god of war, law, and oaths, known for his bravery and sacrifice in the binding of the wolf Fenrir. Hel is the goddess of the underworld, ruler of the realm of the dead, also called Hel.

ODIN & FREYR

Odin and Freyr are prominent figures in Norse mythology. Odin, the Allfather, is a god of wisdom, war, death, magic, and poetry, while Freyr, of the Vanir tribe, is a god of peace, prosperity, and fertility. Odin is associated with Valhalla, where he welcomes fallen warriors, and Freyr is connected to good harvests, sunshine, and fair weather

FREYA & THOR

In Norse mythology, Thor is the god of thunder, strength, and protection, while Freya is the goddess of love, beauty, and fertility. Thor is part of the Aesir tribe, while Freya is from the Vanir tribe, but she later resides with the Aesir after the Aesir-Vanir war. While not directly related by blood, they are part of the same divine family, with Freya sometimes referred to as Thor's stepmother.

LOKI & BALDUR

In Norse mythology, Loki, the trickster god, is infamous for orchestrating the death of Baldr, the god of light and purity. Loki tricked Baldr's blind brother, Höðr, into throwing a mistletoe dart at Baldr, which was the only thing that could harm him, as Frigg had made everything else swear not to harm him. This act of deception and murder cemented Loki's reputation as a chaotic and malicious force.

FRIGG & ODEN

Frigg and Odin are prominent figures in Norse mythology. Frigg is the wife of Odin and the queen of the Æsir, known for her association with marriage, motherhood, and prophecy. Odin, the Allfather, is the god of wisdom, war, and death, often depicted with one eye and associated with ravens and Valhalla.
