From Ra to Osiris: Exploring the Egyptian Pantheon

Creation Of The Universe

In the beginning, there was only Nun, the endless, dark, chaotic primordial waters. There was no land, no sky, and no life — just this infinite watery abyss. From Nun, a mound of dry land emerged. This mound was called the Benben, and it symbolized the first piece of earth. This reflects how the Nile’s annual floods receded to reveal fertile land — a cycle that inspired the myth. Different Egyptian cities told the creation story with their own main god or goddess.

The most well-known versions are:

Heliopolitan Myth

The sun god Atum (sometimes identified with Ra) created himself from Nun. Atum stood on the Benben and produced the first divine couple by spitting or sneezing them out: Shu (air) and Tefnut (moisture).

Memphite Myth

Here, the creator was Ptah, the god of craftsmen. Ptah created the world by speaking it into existence — he conceived the universe in his heart and brought it forth through his tongue.

Hermopolitan Myth

Here, creation began with Eight Primordial Deities called the Ogdoad — four male-female pairs representing darkness, hiddenness, endlessness, and the watery abyss.

Underworld - Afterlife

Duat

The soul's journey began in Duat, the mysterious underworld.

The sun god Ra traveled through Duat each night, fighting Apophis to be reborn at dawn.

The Ka and Ba, once judged worthy, could unite to become the eternal Akh.

Symbols and Objects

The Ankh

The Egyptian ankh, one of the most recognizable symbols in ancient Egyptian culture, has captivated the imagination of people for centuries. Its distinct looped cross shape has been associated with life, power, and eternity.

The Djed Pillar

The Djed is an ancient Egyptian symbol for stability known as “The Backbone of Osiris". This symbol was Osirian in nature and was primarily associated with themes of rebirth and regeneration.

The Eye of Ra

There are different myths about the origin of the Eye of Ra symbol. However, most experts believe that the symbol was actually the right eye of Horus and became known as the Eye of Ra in ancient times. According to various myths, the Eye of Ra was personified by goddesses like Wadjet, Hathor, Mut, Sekhmet, and Bastet.

Eye of Horus

The Eye of Horus, also known as the Eye of Ra, was used in ancient Egypt to protect against envy, disease, harmful animals, and evil spirits. It also symbolized good health and royal power. It represents both sacrificial and healing powers, and was often made of gold in the form of the god Re.

Mythical Creatures

Sphinx

Traditionally depicted with the body of a lion and the head of a human, the Sphinx represents strength and wisdom.

Ammit

The Devourer of the Dead, is a fearsome creature with a lioness's head, a hippopotamus's body, and a crocodile's hindquarters.

Apep

A giant serpent or dragon, Apep was the embodiment of chaos and evil, constantly threatening the order of the world

Bennu

It was believed that the Bennu bird played a role in the creation of the world and represented the eternal cycle of life, death, and rebirth.

Griffin

The body of a lion and the head and wings of an eagle. This hybrid guardian symbolised divine protection and power.

Abtu

Egyptian goddess Isis is said to have transformed into a sacred fish by the name of Abtu.

COSMIC STRUGGLES -

Isfet or Asfet (meaning "injustice", "chaos", or "violence"; as a verb, “to do evil”) is an ancient Egyptian term from Egyptian mythology used in philosophy, which was built on a religious, social and politically affected dualism.
Isfet was the counter to Maat, which was order. Isfet did not have a physical form. Rather, it was believed that Isfet was personified in the form of Apep. Isfet was important in Egyptian culture as Isfet showed that there is balance in the world.
The principles of the contrariness between Isfet and Ma'at are exemplified in a popular tale from the Middle Kingdom, called The Moaning of the Bedouin:

In the eyes of the Egyptians, the world was always ambiguous; the actions and judgments of a king were thought to simplify these principles in order to keep Ma'at by separating Order from Chaos or Good from Evil.
Coffin Text 335a asserts the necessity of the dead being cleansed of isfet in order to be reborn in the Duat.
Isfet is thought to be the product of an individual's free will rather than a primordial state of chaos. In mythology, this is represented by Apep being born from Ra's umbilical cord relatively late.
